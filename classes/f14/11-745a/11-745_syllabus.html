Advanced Statistical Learning Seminar (11-745)

Spring 2015 Special Topic: Foundations of Machine Learning (6 Units)

Description: This seminar aims to deepen the participants' understanding of the theoretical foundation of statistical learning and applications, to broaden their knowledge of new techniques, directions and challenges in the field, and to inspire research ideas through class-room discussions.  In the past years, this seminar was structured as a 12-unit course in the form of group-reading, presenting and discussing the book (chapter by chapter) of The Elements of Statistical Learning: Data Mining, Inference, and Prediction by Trevor Hastie et al. In the spring of 2015, we will have a 6-unit seminar, focusing on selected chapters in Foundations of Machine Learning (Mehryar Mohri, Afsgub Rostanizadeh & Ameet Talwalkar), including PAC Learning, Rademacher Complexity, Margin Theory, Learning Guarantees/Bounds, etc.  We will meet once a week, one topic per week (or two weeks if needed), with presentations rotating among participants. In each week, the assigned presenter starts with the questions from all the participants (collected by email) about the current topic, followed by a presentation on that topic and leads the discussion. All the students are required to read the assigned paper(s) of the week before the class, and email their questions to the presenter (CC to everybody). The slides of each presentation should be shared (by email) with all the class members after the presentation (in the same day). By the end of the semester, each student will individually write a short 3-4-page white paper, outlining a research proposal for new work extending one of the research areas covered in class, or analyzing more than one area with respect to the open challenges, the state-of-the-art sollutions and the future research opportunities. There will be no exams or homework.  The grading is based on class participation (30%), questions submitted for each class and class-room discussions (20%), quality of the seminar presentations (30%) and the final paper (20%).

Prerequisites: 10-701 (PhD-level Machine Learning) or equivalent course is required.  Other relevant courses include 10-702 (Statiscial Machine Learning), 10-705/36-705 (Intermediate Statistics) and 10-725 (Convex Optimization), which will be helpful but not required.  If you are not sure about the expectation, please discuss with the instructor.

As the first time we are going throulgh this book, we are having a conservative schedule, i.e., two weeks per chapter since Chapter 4.  As the course proceeds, if one week per chapter were enough, we could adjust the phase as the following:

1)    Replace the 2nd slot of "On-line learning" with "Stochastic optimization".  [1] has an asymptotic analysis about the convergence and trade-off of stochastic gradient descent, which is related but more useful than online algorithms. (the SGD has also led to the state-of-the-art online SVM solver [2])

2)    Replace the 2nd slot of "Multi-class classification" with "Structural SVMs" [3], which is a pretty widely used generalization of multi-class classifiers. This paper can also serve as an introduction to approximation algorithms such as the cutting plane methods, bundle methods, etc.

3)    Replace the 2nd "Regression" with "Non-parametric Regression" (since we are all more or less familiar with the parametric regression). We can pick a chapter from Larry's book [4]. Alternatively, we can go over Gaussian Process for regression, i.e. the Chapter 2 of [5].
